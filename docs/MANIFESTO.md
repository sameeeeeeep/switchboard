# The Last Prompt

**Does superintelligence feel like it isn't here yet?**

Ask a different question: here for *whom*?

Somewhere right now, someone who never learned to code is shipping working software. Someone who never studied design is producing work an agency would have billed a week for. Someone is running a one-person company that behaves like it has a staff of ten.

And someone else — most people — is typing "make it better" into a chat box, getting slop back, and concluding the whole thing is hype.

Same models. Same day. The difference isn't intelligence. It's fluency.

Here is the paradox of this decade: **AI makes it possible to bypass acquiring skill — but using it is a skill in itself.** You no longer need ten years to learn to code, design, or write. The skill you skipped doesn't disappear, though. It changes shape: knowing what to ask for, prompting it precisely, arming the model with the right skills, sharing the right context, getting used to its interface, and knowing good output from plausible output.

Getting the best out of frontier intelligence means knowing how to use it. Which means superintelligence isn't arriving evenly, like weather. It's pooling — around the fluent. The future is here; it's just gated behind a craft almost nobody has had time to learn.

**The Last Prompt is an AI lab that bridges that gap.**

Not by teaching you to prompt. By doing the work once, properly, and packaging it. For each real task, the lab's job is to find the last prompt — the exact prompts, the sequence, the tools, the taste — the one an expert already wrote, so you never have to.

**Introducing wrapps.**

Yes — wrappers. We wear the word proudly. A wrapp is a series of prompts, skills, workflows, and an interface that completes one task, end to end. "Just a wrapper" was an insult about middleman economics: their model, your card, their markup. Delete the middleman and what remains is the craft — which was the valuable part all along. And the people who found the craft get paid the way artists do: by how much their work is used, not by how well they gatekeep it.

**And one more thing.**

Wrapps run private to you. On your own AI setup. At no extra cost. Without installation. Without signing up.

That sentence should sound impossible. Every app you've ever used needed a server, an account, a key, a bill. So here's the machinery that makes it true:

A small broker lives on your machine. It holds your AI — your Claude subscription, or a model running locally — plus your files, your tools, your context. A wrapp is just static files: a pure frontend that borrows the intelligence you already own. The site never sees a key. You never make an account. Nobody pays for your inference twice. Local by default, private by default; the cloud only where you choose it. And nothing writes, sends, or spends without a human click that no page and no model output can forge. All of it open source, because trust you can't inspect isn't trust.

**Introducing Switchboard.**

The lab finds the craft. Switchboard delivers it — running on your own intelligence, fed by your own context, under your own consent.

Superintelligence, for you, was never going to be a model release. It's fluency, delivered. The last prompt is the one you never have to write.

Operator, patch me through.
